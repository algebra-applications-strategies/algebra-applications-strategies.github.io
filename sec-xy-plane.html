    Section 2.1 The \(xy\)-plane

    Objectives

    In this section, we learn how to:
    🔗
    1. Plot points in the \(xy\)-plane.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find the distance between two points.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Find the midpoint of two given points.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Interpret graphs in context.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Why bother with the \(xy\)-plane?

    It is rare to have an application where we only deal with one quantity (one number) at a time. Often, the value of one quantity has a relationship to the value of another quantity.
    1. For example, suppose you are driving on a road where travel at \(60\) miles per hour. If \(x\) represents the number of hours you have been driving and \(y\) represents the number of miles you have traveled, then we have the relationship \(y = 60x\text{.}\) Having an equation like this involving two variables \(x\) and \(y\) allows us to describe the specific connection between these two quantities. In this example, we will later see that the point \((x,y) = (31,1860)\) is relevant for the equation \(y=60x\text{,}\) and this means that having this point be relevant means that if you drove a total of \(31\) hours on a road trip, then you would have traveled a total of \(1860\) miles.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. As another example, suppose you need to track the height of a plant as it grows. If \(x\) represents the number of weeks since you planted the seed and \(y\) represents the height of the plant in inches, the relationship between these two quantities might be described by the equation \(y = 0.04x^2\) mentioning both variables \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) In this example, the point \((x,y) = (10,4)\) is relevant for the equation \(y = 0.04x^2\text{,}\) which means that \(10\) weeks after planting the seed, the height of the plant is \(4\) inches. The point \((x,y) = (30,36)\) is also relevant, which means that \(30\) weeks after planting the seed, the height of the plant is \(36\) inches.
      🔗
      🔗
    The last example hints at the power of having an equation with two variables. We can plug in a number for \(x\) and get a number for \(y\text{.}\) But then we can plug in a different number for \(x\) and get the relevant \(y\)-value for this new \(x\)-value. The idea that each \(x\)-value leads to a different \(y\)-value means that this relationship can be described algebraically with an equation, but also geometrically with a graph on the \(xy\)-plane.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The \(xy\)-plane

    The \(xy\)-plane (also called the Cartesian plane) has two lines (which are called axes) that are perpendicular to each other. The horizontal line is called the \(x\)-axis and the vertical line is called the \(y\)-axis. Each axis is labeled with the letter of the variable it represents: the \(x\)-axis is labeled on the right and the \(y\)-axis is labeled at the top. The two axes meet at a point called the origin, which can be labeled with the letter \(O\text{.}\) Here is a minimal picture of the \(xy\)-plane: The axes divide the plane into four regions called quadrants. Quadrant I is the upper right region, Quadrant II is the upper left region, Quadrant III is the lower left region, and Quadrant IV is the lower right region. The axes themselves are not part of any quadrant.
    🔗
    Once the axes are drawn, we can draw points in the plane. A point is communicated by writing what is called an ordered pair. An ordered pair is written in the form \((x,y)\text{,}\) where \(x\) is the \(x\)-coordinate of the point and \(y\) is the \(y\)-coordinate of the point. For clarity, we will always write the \(x\)-coordinate first and the \(y\)-coordinate second (so note that these are in alphabetical order: x followed by y next), with a comma between the numbers to separate, and surrounded in parentheses. The \(x\)-coordinate is a number, and the \(y\)-coordinate is a number. So, when a point is written, we always write a left parenthesis, then a number for the \(x\)-coordinate, then a comma, then a number for the \(y\)-coordinate, and then a right parenthesis. For example, writing \((7,2)\) is an example of a point, and for this point, the \(x\)-coordinate is \(7\) and the \(y\)-coordinate is \(2\text{.}\)
    • The \(x\)-coordinate is the horizontal coordinate, since it tells us how far to the left and right the point is from the origin. If the \(x\)-coordinate is positive, then the point is to the right; if the \(x\)-coordinate is negative, then the point is to the left; and if the \(x\)-coordinate is zero, then we do not need to move to the left or right from the origin, so the point would be on the \(y\)-axis.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The \(y\)-coordinate is the vertical coordinate, since it tells us how far up and down the point is from the origin. If the \(y\)-coordinate is positive, then the point is up; if the \(y\)-coordinate is negative, then the point is down; and if the \(y\)-coordinate is zero, then we do not need to move up or down from the origin, so the point would be on the \(x\)-axis.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Principle 2.1.2. A point requires two coordinates.

    Describing a point requires two numbers: the \(x\)-coordinate and the \(y\)-coordinate. The \(x\)-coordinate tells us how much to move horizontally, and the \(y\)-coordinate tells us how much to move vertically.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.3.

    To plot the point \((7,2)\text{,}\) we start at the origin and move to the right (because the \(x\)-coordinate is positive) a distance of \(7\text{,}\) and then we move up (because the \(y\)-coordinate is positive) a distance of \(2\text{.}\) This point is in Quadrant I.
    🔗
    To plot the point \((-3,4)\text{,}\) we start at the origin and move to the left \(3\) units, and then we move up \(4\) units. This point is in Quadrant II.
    🔗
    To plot the point \((-6,-5)\text{,}\) we start at the origin and move to the left \(6\) units, and then we move down \(5\) units. This point is in Quadrant III.
    🔗
    To plot the point \((2,-3)\text{,}\) we start at the origin and move to the right \(2\) units, and then we move down \(3\) units. This point is in Quadrant IV.
    🔗
    To plot the point \((0,8)\text{,}\) we start at the origin and do not move left or right (because the \(x\)-coordinate is zero), and then we move up \(8\) units.
    🔗
    All five of the points we discussed are plotted in Figure Figure 2.1.4. We also included tick marks at \(x=5\) and \(y=5\) to help give a sense of scale. (Instead of providing so few tick marks, we could have provided a tick mark at each unit.)
    🔗
    🔗
    One of the points we plotted above was \((0,8)\text{.}\) Because the \(x\)-coordinate of this point is zero, we did not move to the left or right from the origin, so the point we drew ended up on the on the \(y\)-axis. It always happens that if the \(x\)-coordinate of a point is zero, then the point is on the \(y\)-axis:
    🔗

    Principle 2.1.5. A point on the \(y\)-axis.

    A point is on the \(y\)-axis if and only if its \(x\)-coordinate is zero. (Recall that the \(x\)-coordinate is the horizontal coordinate, and when \(x=0\text{,}\) there is no horizontal movement relative to the origin.)
    🔗
    🔗

    Principle 2.1.6. A point on the \(x\)-axis.

    A point is on the \(x\)-axis if and only if its \(y\)-coordinate is zero. (Recall that the \(y\)-coordinate is the vertical coordinate, and when \(y=0\text{,}\) there is no vertical movement relative to the origin.)
    🔗
    🔗
    If we are asked to graph one or more points and they are all in the same quadrant, we can draw our axes in such a way that only the relevant quadrant is visible. For example, if all the points we are asked to plot have no negative coordinates, we can draw our axes in such a way that only Quadrant I is visible.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.7.

    Graph the points \((4,0)\) and \((5,2)\) and \((6,4)\) and \((7,6)\text{.}\) What do you notice about these points?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since there are no negative coordinates, we will draw our axes in such a way that only Quadrant I is visible. (Our earlier graph showed tick marks at \(x=5\) and \(y=5\text{.}\) To show acceptable variations, this time we label tick marks at every unit on both axes.) There are many possible things to notice about these points, but one thing that we might notice is that all of the points that we drew are on the same [straight] line. The reason that all four points are on the same line is due to another possible thing that we might notice: going from one point to the next, the \(x\)-coordinate always increases by \(1\) while the \(y\)-coordinate always increases by \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Distance between points

    Given one point \((x_1,y_1)\) and another point \((x_2,y_2)\text{,}\) we can find the distance between these two points. Because each point has an \(x\)-coordinate, we have labeled \(x_1\) for the \(x\)-coordinate of the first point, and \(x_2\) for the \(x\)-coordinate of the second point. Similarly, because each point has a \(y\)-coordinate, we have labeled \(y_1\) for the \(y\)-coordinate of the first point, and \(y_2\) for the \(y\)-coordinate of the second point.
    🔗
    \(y\)\(x\)\(x\)

    Example 2.1.9.

    • To find the distance between \((3,8)\) and \((7,8)\text{,}\) since the two points have the same \(y\)-coordinate, the distance is \(7-3=4\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • To find the distance between \((3,8)\) and \((1,8)\text{,}\) since the two points have the same \(y\)-coordinate, the distance is \(3-1=2\text{.}\) Note the order of subtraction: \(3\) is a larger \(x\)-value than \(1\) is.
      🔗
      🔗
    • To find the distance between \((3,6)\) and \((-2,6)\text{,}\) since the two points have the same \(y\)-coordinate, the distance is \(3-(-2)=3+2=5\text{.}\) Note the order of subtraction: \(3\) is a larger \(x\)-value than \(-2\) is. We still subtract the \(x\)-coordinates, even though one of the \(x\)-values is negative.
      🔗
      🔗
    • To find the distance between \((-3,6)\) and \((-7,6)\text{,}\) since the two points have the same \(y\)-coordinate, the distance is \(-3-(-7)=-3+7=4\text{.}\) Note the order of subtraction: \(-3\) is a larger \(x\)-value than \(-7\) is. We still subtract the \(x\)-coordinates, even though both of the \(x\)-values are negative.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.10.

    Suppose we have a first point \((x_1,z)\) and a second point \((x_2,z)\text{.}\) Note that \(z\) is the stated \(y\)-coordinate for both points, so the points would be drawn at the same height. Find the distance between these two points.
    🔗
    Answer.
    We can subtract \(x\)-values to find the distance. If \(x_1\) is larger than \(x_2\text{,}\) then the distance is \(x_1-x_2\text{.}\) If \(x_2\) is larger than \(x_1\text{,}\) then the distance is \(x_2-x_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    When two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate, the distance between them is the absolute value of the difference of their \(y\)-coordinates.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.11.

    Find the distance between \((8,9)\) and \((8,6)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate, the distance is \(9-6=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.12.

    Find the distance between \((8,9)\) and \((8,16)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate, the distance is \(16-9=7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.13.

    Find the distance between \((8,9)\) and \((8,-6)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate, the distance is \(9-(-6)=9+6=15\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.14.

    Find the distance between \((8,y_1)\) and \((8,y_2)\text{,}\) under the assumption \(y_2>y_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate, the distance is \(y_2-y_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.15.

    Find the distance between \((z,y_1)\) and \((z,y_2)\text{,}\) under the assumption \(y_2>y_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate (which was labeled with the letter \(z\) to represent a mystery value), the distance is \(y_2-y_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Our examples so far described how to get the distance between two points that have the same \(x\)-coordinate or two points that have the same \(y\)-coordinate. Now, we can do a short exploration to build a formula for the distance between two points that have different \(x\)-coordinates and different \(y\)-coordinates. This exploration will rely on what we already know about the distance when there are matching coordinates.
    🔗
    To start, let’s say we have a point called \(A\) with coordinates \((x_1,y_1)\) and a point called \(B\) with coordinates \((x_2,y_2)\text{.}\) We will look at a generic picture that assumes \(x_2>x_1\) and \(y_2>y_1\text{.}\) Here is an initial picture. To contiue our exploration, from point \(A\) we draw perfectly horizontally to the right, and from point \(B\) we draw perfectly vertically down. The additional drawing leads to a point called \(C\text{,}\) which has coordinates \((x_2,y_1)\text{.}\) Here is the updated picture. This builds a right triangle, and we can label the lengths of the sides \(a,b,c\text{.}\) Note that the Pythagorean Theorem \(a^2+b^2=c^2\) applies to this right triangle. Since the vertical side \(a\) represents the distance between two points with the same \(x\)-coordinate, we know that \(a=y_2-y_1\text{,}\) just like in Example Example 2.1.15. Since the horizontal side \(b\) represents the distance between two points with the same \(y\)-coordinate, we know that \(b=x_2-x_1\text{,}\) just like in Example Example 2.1.10. By replacing \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) we have \(c^2 = (x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2\text{.}\) Square rooting both sides, \(c=\pm\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\text{.}\) Distances cannot be negative, so \(c=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Principle 2.1.19.

    The distance between \((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\) is \(d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We saw that squaring does not distribute across minus signs in Warning Warning 1.5.50, and we also saw that radicals do not distribute over addition in Warning Warning 1.5.113), so this formula does not simplify any further than as currently written. In particular, it is an error to turn \(\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\) into either \(\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2}+\sqrt{(y_2-y_1)^2}\) or \((x_2-x_1)+(y_2-y_1)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Warning 2.1.20.

    The distance formula does not simplify to either \(\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2}+\sqrt{(y_2-y_1)^2}\) or \((x_2-x_1)+(y_2-y_1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we have finished the exploration, we can use the formula to quickly find the distance between any two points in the \(xy\)-plane.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.21.

    Find the distance between \((3,8)\) and \((10,2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Using the distance formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} d = \sqrt{(10-3)^2+(2-8)^2} = = \sqrt{7^2 + (-6)^2} = \sqrt{49+36} = \sqrt{85}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that we cannot turn \(\sqrt{(10-3)^2+(2-8)^2}\) into \(\sqrt{(10-3)^2}+\sqrt{(2-8)^2}\) or \((10-3)+(2-8)\text{.}\) (That is, Warning Warning 2.1.20 applies also when we have substituted values.) In addition, \(\sqrt{49+36}\) does not simplify to \(\sqrt{49}+\sqrt{36}\) or \(7+6\) because of Warning Warning 1.5.113.
    🔗
    Please note that we wrote \(\sqrt{7^2+(-6)^2}\text{,}\) which is not the same as \(\sqrt{7^2-6^2}\text{.}\) When we are squaring a negative number, we need to insert parentheses around the negative number due to the Order of Operations.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.22.

    Find the distance between \((4,6)\) and \((3,20)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Using the distance formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} d = \sqrt{(3-4)^2+(20-6)^2} = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + 14^2} = \sqrt{1+196} = \sqrt{197}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that we cannot turn \(\sqrt{(3-4)^2+(20-6)^2}\) into \(\sqrt{(3-4)^2}+\sqrt{(20-6)^2}\) or \((3-4)+(20-6)\text{.}\) In addition, \(\sqrt{1+196}\) does not simplify to \(\sqrt{1}+\sqrt{196}\) or \(1+14\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.23.

    Find the distance between \((-3,6)\) and \((4,-2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Using the distance formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} d = \sqrt{(4-(-3))^2+(-2-6)^2} = \sqrt{7^2 + (-8)^2} = \sqrt{49+64} = \sqrt{113}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Even though the first coordinate of the first point is negative, we still subtract \(-3\) from \(4\) to get \(4-(-3)=7\text{.}\) (In other words, we subtract one \(x\)-coordinate from the other \(x\)-coordinate, regardless of whether any of the \(x\)-coordinates is negative.)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.24.

    Find the distance between \((7,9)\) and \((7,6)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    Using the distance formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} d = \sqrt{(7-7)^2+(6-9)^2} = \sqrt{0^2 + (-3)^2} = \sqrt{0+9} = \sqrt{9} = 3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    Since the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate, by following Example Example 2.1.15, by subtracting the larger \(y\)-coordinate from the smaller \(y\)-coordinate: \(9-6=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Midpoint

    Given one point \((x_1,y_1)\) and another point \((x_2,y_2)\text{,}\) we can find the midpoint between these two points. The midpoint is the point that is exactly halfway between the two points. To be more specific, if we connect the two points with a straight line, the midpoint is the point on that line that is exactly halfway between the two points.
    🔗

    Principle 2.1.26. Midpoint formula.

    The midpoint of the point \((x_1,y_1)\) and the point \((x_2,y_2)\) is the point
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    To find the midpoint, we can take the average of the \(x\)-coordinates and the average of the \(y\)-coordinates. Keep in mind that the midpoint is a point, so the \(x\)-coordinate and the \(y\)-coordinate should be separated by a comma, and all of this data should be enclosed in parentheses.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.27.

    Find the midpoint of \((4,8)\) and \((10,12)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Using the midpoint formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{4+10}{2},\frac{8+12}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{14}{2},\frac{20}{2}\right) = (7,10). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.28.

    Find the midpoint of \((-3,6)\) and \((4,2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Using the midpoint formula, we have
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{-3+4}{2},\frac{6+2}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{2},\frac{8}{2}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{2},4\right). \end{equation*}
    We still average the \(x\)-coordinates (by adding the two \(x\)-coordinates and dividing by \(2\)) even though one of the \(x\)-coordinates is negative.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Application: Interpreting graphs in context

    In this section, we explain how to interpret graphs in real-world contexts. To begin, we may be given a graph like this along with a description of what the \(x\) variable means and what \(y\) variable means. Given the graph and the description of the variables, we can provide an interpretation that specifically discusses what the variables mean.
    🔗
    To warm up, we discuss this graph without introducing meanings for the \(x\) and \(y\) variables. In the graph above, we see a line drawn from the point \((4,1)\) to the point \((1,4)\text{.}\) (For clarification, when we say "graph", we are talking about this line segment, and the axes themselves are not points on the graph: the axes just help clarify the location of points.) Start by picking a point on the middle of the graph. In other words, pick a point between \((4,1)\) and \((1,4)\text{.}\) Starting at a point on the graph and moving in any of the four cardinal directions (up, down, left, right), we would not stay on the graph. If we were to try to move to the right, we should also move down in order to stay on the graph. To summarize our options, starting from a point on the graph
    1. If we move right, we must also move down.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If we move left, we must also move up.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If we move down, we must also move right.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. If we move up, we must also move left.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Recall that the \(x\)-coordinate is the horizontal coordinate, and bigger \(x\)-coordinates are to the right, so smaller \(x\)-coordinates are to the left:

    Principle 2.1.30.

    • Moving to the right means \(x\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Moving to the left means \(x\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Similarly, the \(y\)-coordinate is the vertical coordinate, and bigger \(y\)-coordinates are higher, so smaller \(y\)-coordinates are lower:

    Principle 2.1.31.

    • Moving up means \(y\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Moving down means \(y\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Based on these principles, we can rewrite each of the four options in the earlier numbered list. Following the same order:
    1. If \(x\) increases, then \(y\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(x\) decreases, then \(y\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(y\) decreases, then \(x\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. If \(y\) increases, then \(x\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    With these four texts getting shorter, let’s point out that the first and third statement are saying the same thing. Both mention \(x\) increases and \(y\) decreases. So, we don’t need to include both. Similarly, the second and fourth statements are saying the same thing. Both mention \(x\) decreases and \(y\) increases. In the future, to avoid any need repeating the information in different language, we make the decision now to mention the letters in alphabetical order. We will talk about \(x\) first, and then \(y\) next. So, our list is shortened to two statements:
    1. If \(x\) increases, then \(y\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(x\) decreases, then \(y\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    Now, we should notice that both statement are still saying the same thing. One is just talking about moving up and to the left, while the other is talking about moving down and to the right. If you pull up a map of South Korea, you’ll see that Seoul is northwest of Busan, so Busan is southeast of Seoul. The two cities are connected by National Highway 1, and while it is not straight, overall it has the same direction as the line drawn in Figure Figure 2.1.29. Driving from Seoul to Busan, you drive primarily southeast, so if we placed \(xy\)-axes on the map, as \(x\) increases, \(y\) decreases. If you drive from Busan to Seoul, you drive northwest: as \(x\) decreases, \(y\) increases. So, in the end, we make the convention to start by talking about \(x\) first, and specifically about \(x\) increasing. Then we examine the graph to see what happens to \(y\text{.}\) We describe whether \(y\) increases or decreases in order to stay on the graph. That is, as we travel to the right (so \(x\) increases), we describe whether \(y\) increases or decreases (do we also travel up or down). In this case:
    1. If \(x\) increases, then \(y\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Let’s compare this to the following graph: In this graph,
    1. If \(x\) increases, then \(y\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(x\) decreases, then \(y\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(y\) decreases, then \(x\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. If \(y\) increases, then \(x\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    Just like before, the third statement has the same information as the first statement. The fourth statement says the same as the second statement. We shorten the list by only keeping the statements that talk about \(x\) first:
    1. If \(x\) increases, then \(y\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(x\) decreases, then \(y\) decreases.
      🔗
      🔗
    Both of these statements say the same thing. If you examine a map of Texas, you will see that, relative to each other, San Antonio is southwest of Austin, so Austin is northeast of San Antonio. Driving on Interstate 35 from San Antonio to Austin, you drive primarily northeast, so if we placed \(xy\)-axes on the map, as \(x\) increases, \(y\) increases. If you drive from Austin to San Antonio, you drive southwest: as \(x\) decreases, \(y\) decreases. Recall earlier we will keep the convention to start by talking about \(x\) first, and specifically about \(x\) increasing. Then we examine the graph to see what happens to \(y\text{.}\) In this case:
    1. If \(x\) increases, then \(y\) increases.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    As we analyze the graph of a line, we discuss \(x\) first, specifically about \(x\) increasing. Then, we ask to stay on the line, do we need to also move up or down. If we need to move up as we move right, then \(y\) increases. If we need to move down, then \(y\) decreases.
    🔗
    Finally, we perform this activity in the context of having each axis labeled with a quantity. For example, the \(y\)-coordinate might represent the temperature of a cat. Then \(y\) increasing in this context would mean the cat gets hotter (or the cat gets warmer), and \(y\) decreasing would mean the cat gets colder (or the cat gets cooler). When we answer questions about graph interpretation, our response should be accurate but also use language that is specific to the labelings on each axis.

    🔗

    We should also use comparative words to make the language plain and simple. (A comparative word typically ends in the letters "er". For example: hotter, colder, faster, slower, taller, shorter.) In Example 2.1.34, saying "As the distance of the cat from City Hall increases, the temperature of the cat decreases" accurately describes what is happening, but writing writing the words "increases" and "decreases" (or substitutes such as "bigger" and "smaller") makes the sentence longer, and it would be harder to understand for younger people. When we use comparative words, we create language that would be easier for, say, someone in elementary school. Moreover, whenever concepts can be described to someone in elementary school, forcing ourselves to do this shows that we really understand! This is why we wrote "As the cat gets further from City Hall, the cat gets colder" instead of "As the distance of the cat from City Hall increases, the temperature of the cat decreases." If we wanted to get to the point (and maybe leave out the mention of the cat), we could even say "Further means colder". Similarly, for Example 2.1.36, we could say "Hotter means slower".

    Principle 2.1.38. Expectation.

    When interpreting a graph, use comparative words that are specific to what each variable means. (Instead of using generic words such as "increases" and "decreases" and "bigger" and "smaller", use words such as "hotter", "colder", "faster", "slower", etc. where possible.)

    Subsection Summary

    1. Each point in the \(xy\)-plane consists of two numbers: an \(x\)-value and a \(y\)-value, formatted as \((x, y)\) with parentheses.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The \(x\)-axis represents the horizontal direction, and the \(y\)-axis represents the vertical direction. Larger \(x\)-values are to the right, and larger \(y\)-values are higher.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The distance between the point \((x_1,y_1)\) and the point \((x_2,y_2)\) is given \(\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\text{,}\) and the square root does not distribute either before or after plugging in numbers. A simplified formula is available if the two points have the same \(x\)-coordinate or the same \(y\)-coordinate: larger value minus smaller value.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The midpoint of the line segment connecting the points \((x_1,y_1)\) and \((x_2,y_2)\) is given by \(\left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\text{.}\) The resulting point is the result of averaging the \(x\)-coordinates and averaging the \(y\)-coordinates.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Many points together may come together to form a graph, such as a line or curve. We interpret graphs by describing what happens to \(y\) as \(x\) increases, and we use language that is specific to the variables, and write with comparative words (words ending in "er") whenever possible.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
