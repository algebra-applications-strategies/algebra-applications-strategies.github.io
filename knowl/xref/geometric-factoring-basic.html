Strategy 1.6.42.

Recall from Principle 1.1.40 that addition can be represented by gluing sticks together, and from Principle 1.1.54 that multiplication can be represented by area of a rectangle. To factor \(x^2+Bx+C\text{,}\) draw a rectangle and divide it into four smaller rectangles. Label the first side vertically and the first side horizontally with \(x\text{.}\) Label the top left rectangle with \(x^2\) and the bottom right rectangle with \(C\text{.}\) The second side vertically and the second side horizontally need to be filled in with numbers so that the two numbers multiply to \(C\) and the two unlabeled areas add up to \(Bx\text{.}\) While our description above the figure is accurate, it is easier to do repackage it by saying that we are looking for two expressions to multiply to \(Cx^2\) and add to \(Bx\text{.}\) Here’s a practical way to do this: Consider the expression \(C \cdot x^2\text{.}\) Write down all pairs of factors that multiply to \(C \cdot x^2\text{,}\) with an \(x\) appearing in each factor. Then, among this list of pairs, find the pair that adds to \(Bx\text{.}\) It is often helpful to write all the factor pairs of \(C\text{,}\) and then go through the list to see which pair of numbers adds to \(B\text{.}\)
