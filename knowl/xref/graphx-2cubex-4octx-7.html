Example 3.4.34.

Graph the polynomial function \(f(x) = (x+2)^3(x-4)^8(x-7)\text{.}\)
🔗
Answer.
The roots are \(x=-2,4,7\) with multiplicities \(3,8,1\text{,}\) respectively. The graph will cross the \(x\)-axis at \(x=-2\) and \(x=7\text{,}\) and will bounce at \(x=4\text{.}\) These three locations (the roots) are the only lactions where the graph intersects the \(x\)-axis.
🔗
We build a table of signs, noting that having cuts at \(-2,4,7\) generates four pieces: \((-\infty,-2)\text{,}\) \((-2,4)\text{,}\) \((4,7)\text{,}\) and \((7,\infty)\text{.}\) Notice that in the row for the factor \((x-4)^8\text{,}\) we have a \(+\) in each column, because the factor is always positive: an even power guarantees that the value is never negative.
🔗
Scanning the bottom row of the table (the row for \(f(x)\)) tells us what the sketch of the graph of the polynomial function looks like. The plus shown first indicates that the graph starts above the \(x\)-axis for values of \(x\) in \((-\infty,-2)\text{.}\) Then the graph has its first \(x\)-intercept at \(x=-2\text{,}\) and because the multiplicity of this root is odd, the graph crosses the \(x\)-axis at this root. The next interval is \((-2,4)\text{,}\) and the graph is below the \(x\)-axis for values of \(x\) in this interval, which is what the \(-\) sign indicates. The next \(x\)-intercept is at \(x=4\text{,}\) and because the multiplicity of this root is even, the graph bounces at this root and stays below the \(x\)-axis for values of \(x\) in \((4,7)\text{,}\) which also see in the \(-\) sign in the bottom row of the table. The last \(x\)-intercept is at \(x=7\text{,}\) and because the multiplicity of this root is odd, the graph crosses the \(x\)-axis at this root and the graph is above the \(x\)-axis for values of \(x\) in \((7,\infty)\text{,}\) which is what the \(+\) sign indicates.
