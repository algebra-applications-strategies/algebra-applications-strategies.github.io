    Section 2.3 Quadratic Equations: factoring

    Objectives

    In this section, we learn how to:
    🔗
    1. Solve quadratic equations by factoring.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Zero Product Property

    Recall a general idea discussed earlier in Strategy 1.3.43:when solving an equation where the variable appears more than once, we need to factor. (On a practical level, we need to factor because we need to get to place where the variable is only written once.) Even in a problem like \(3x+8x=25\text{,}\) the step of combinining like terms and getting \(11x=25\) is a form of factoring: Starting from \(3x+8x=25\text{,}\) we can factor out the \(x\) to get \((3+8)x=25\text{,}\) which becomes \(11x=25\text{.}\) We look at applying factoring to solve new types of equations called quadratic equations.
    🔗
    This section is about solving quadratic equations by factoring. By a quadratic equation, we mean that we have an equation (so there is an equal sign), and we only have terms that are constants (that is, numbers without variables), terms that are linear (that is, terms with a variable raised to the first power), and terms that are quadratic (that is, terms with a variable raised to the second power). In other words, we have an equation where the highest power of the variable is \(2\text{.}\) Examples of quadratic equations include \(x^2-9=0\) and \(x^2+3x-4=0\) and \(2x^2-5x+3=0\) and \(x^2+3x-4=5\) and \(4x^2-8x+9=3x^2+2x+8\text{.}\) Examples of equations that are not quadratic include \(x^3-9=0\) and \(2x^3-5x+3=7\text{:}\) in both of these examples, the highest power is \(3\text{,}\) so these are cubic equations, not quadratic equations.

    Principle 2.3.1. The Zero Product Property.

    If \(a \cdot b = 0\text{,}\) then \(a=0\) or \(b=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In words, say we are in the situation that we have an equation, where one side is a product of two quantities (labeled \(a\) and \(b\) above), while the other side is zero. Then we can conclude that at least one of the two quantities must be zero.

    Principle 2.3.2. How to use the Zero Product Property.

    First ensure that we are in the situation described by \(a \cdot b = 0\text{.}\)
    1. Check that we have an equation to solve, not an expression to simplify.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Check that one side of the equation is a product. In other words, this side should be the result of multiplying two quantities together (we have labeled these factors \(a\) and \(b\) above). We have a product if reading the expression on this side of the equation according to Order of Operations, a multiplication that is present in the expression is the last operation that would be performed.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Check that the other side of the equation is zero.
      🔗
      🔗
    After verifying all three conditions, one line later, write two equations on the same line with the word "or" in between: the equations we write are each factor is set equal to zero.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.3.

    Solve the equation \((x+8)(x-20)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We have an equation. One side is a product, since the multiplication is the last operation to be performed. The product has factors \(x+8\) and \(x-20\text{.}\) The other side of the equation is zero. Therefore, the Zero Product Property applies, and our next line of work to write is
    \begin{equation*} x+8=0 \text{ or } x-20=0. \end{equation*}
    Subtract \(8\) on both sides of the first equation and add \(20\) on both sides of the second equation to get
    \begin{equation*} x=-8 \text{ or } x=20. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    We can check our work. If we substitute \(x=-8\) into the original equation, the right side is already \(0\) without doing any substitutions (there are no \(x\)’s to substitute on the right side), while the left side is \((-8+8)(-8-20)=0\cdot(-28)=0\text{,}\) so the equation is satisfied. If we substitute \(x=20\) into the original equation, the right side is still \(0\text{,}\) while the left side is \((20+8)(20-20)=28\cdot0=0\text{,}\) so the equation is satisfied.

    Example 2.3.4.

    Solve the equation \((2x+1)(x-3)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We have an equation where one side is a product and the other side is zero. By the Zero Product Property,
    \begin{equation*} 2x+1=0 \text{ or } x-3=0. \end{equation*}
    Subtract \(1\) on both sides of the first equation and add \(3\) on both sides of the second equation:
    \begin{equation*} 2x=-1 \text{ or } x=3. \end{equation*}
    Divide both sides of the first equation by \(2\) and leave the second equation alone:
    \begin{equation*} x=-\frac{1}{2} \text{ or } x=3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    It is important to not fall into the trap of thinking that the Zero Product Property only applies when each factor is a binomial. The factors can be any expressions: as long as one side is a product and the other side is zero, the Zero Product Property applies. This example helps illustrate this point.

    Example 2.3.5.

    Solve the equation \(x(x-7)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We have an equation where one side is a product and the other side is zero. The factors are \(x\) and \(x-7\text{.}\) If you wish to have more convincing, you can write the equation as \((x) \cdot (x-7)=0\text{.}\) The point to illustrate here is that it’s not about whether a factor is in parentheses or not: the point to make is that we need factors, the result of multiplication. By the Zero Product Property,
    \begin{equation*} x=0 \text{ or } x-7=0. \end{equation*}
    Add \(7\) on both sides of the second equation to get
    \begin{equation*} x=0 \text{ or } x=7. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Since this feels like an "unusual" example, to convince ourselves that the Zero Product Property applied validly, we can check our work. If we substitute \(x=0\) into the original equation, the right side is already \(0\) without doing any substitutions (there are no \(x\)’s to substitute on the right side), while the left side is \((0)(0-7)=0\cdot(-7)=0\text{,}\) so the equation is satisfied. If we substitute \(x=7\) into the original equation, the right side is still \(0\text{,}\) while the left side is \((7)(7-7)=7\cdot0=0\text{,}\) so the equation is satisfied.

    Warning 2.3.6.

    The Zero Product Property only applies when one side of the equation is a product and the other side is zero. If we have an equation where one side is a product and the other side is not zero, we cannot use the Zero Product Property. For example, if we have \(x(x-1)=20\text{,}\) we cannot conclude that \(x=20\) or \(x-1=20\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Suppose somebody tried this. Then they would conclude \(x=20\) or \(x=21\) by adding \(1\) to both sides of \(x-1=20\text{.}\)
    🔗
    But did this work? That is, is it true that \(x=20\) or \(x=21\) should be our final answer \(x(x-1)=20\text{?}\) With any equation, we can check to see if the left and the right sides equal.
    1. If \(x=20\text{,}\) then the left side is \(20(20-1)=20(19)=380\text{,}\) while the right side is \(20\text{.}\) These are not equal, so \(x=20\) is not a solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(x=21\text{,}\) then the left side is \(21(21-1)=21(20)=420\text{,}\) while the right side is \(20\text{.}\) These are not equal, so \(x=21\) is not a solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The warning above tells us that there is no "Twenty Product Property". The name itself (Zero Product Property) is a helpful reminder. If we don’t see that zero on one side of the equation, we should avoid trying to "split up" an equation at the multiplication sign. For the equation \(x(x-1)=20\text{,}\) what can we do instead?

    Example 2.3.7.

    Solve the equation \(x(x-1)=20\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Distributing on the left side, we have \(x^2-x=20\text{.}\) Subtract \(20\) on both sides to get \(x^2-x-20=0\text{.}\) The left side is an expression which factors. We consider the product of the first term and last term, \(-20 \cdot x^2\text{.}\) Let’s list all pairs of factors of \(-20x^2\) where each factor contains an \(x\text{:}\)
    • \(1x\) and \(-20x\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(2x\) and \(-10x\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(4x\) and \(-5x\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(5x\) and \(-4x\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(10x\) and \(-2x\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(20x\) and \(-1x\)
      🔗
      🔗
    The pair that adds to \(-1x\) is \(4x\) and \(-5x\text{,}\) so we update the area model: Replacing the previous left side \(x^2-x-20\) with the factored form \((x-5)(x+4)\text{,}\) we have \((x-5)(x+4)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x-5=0 \text{ or } x+4=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x=5 \text{ or } x=-4. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Our next example relies on work from an earlier section.

    Example 2.3.10.

    Solve the equation \(x^2+11x+24=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    From the factoring result in Example 1.6.44, we have \((x+3)(x+8)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x+3=0 \text{ or } x+8=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x=-3 \text{ or } x=-8. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    When we had Example 1.6.44 which only asked us to factor the expression \(x^2+11x+24\text{,}\) we added Warning 1.6.49 right after to just factor if the task was to factor an expression, and promised that a later section would discuss how to use factoring to solve an equation. This is that moment. This discussion doesn’t really have to be memorized on its own: it’s part of the bigger discussion of keeping track of what the task is asking, and remembering the difference between an expression and an equation. When the math notation given to us is an expression, we can only rewrite it in different forms (by simplifying or factoring, for example), but we cannot solve it. The task of solving is left to equations: when we start with an equation, a final answer is in the format of a solved equation: one or more equations of the format where the variable is alone on one side, and a number (without the variable) is alone on the other side.

    Example 2.3.11.

    Solve the equation \(x^2-12x+35\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    From the factoring result in Example 1.6.50, we have \((x-5)(x-7)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x-5=0 \text{ or } x-7=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x=5 \text{ or } x=7. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.12.

    Solve the equation \(x^2+6x+9=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    From the factoring result in Example 1.6.66, we have \((x+3)(x+3)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x+3=0 \text{ or } x+3=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x=-3 \text{ or } x=-3. \end{equation*}
    We can leave our final answer as is, or write this as a single solution:
    \begin{equation*} x=-3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.13.

    Solve the equation \(x^2-9=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    By factoring the left side, \((x-3)(x+3)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x-3=0 \text{ or } x+3=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x=3 \text{ or } x=-3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.14.

    Solve the equation \(x^3+5x^2+2x+10=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    In Example 1.6.94, we factored the expression on the left side using Factoring By Grouping. Borriwng from that work, \((x^2+2)(x+5)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x^2+2=0 \text{ or } x+5=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x^2=-2 \text{ or } x=-5. \end{equation*}
    The first equation has no real solutions, so our only real solution is
    \begin{equation*} x=-5. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    While this section focuses on solving quadratic equations, the technique of factoring (by grouping) together with the Zero Product Property allowed us to solve this cubic equation.

    Example 2.3.15.

    Solve the equation \(6x^2-7x-20=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    In Example 1.6.119, we factored the expression on the left side. Borrowing from that work, \((3x+4)(2x-5)=0\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} 3x+4=0 \text{ or } 2x-5=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} 3x=-4 \text{ or } 2x=5. \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides of the first equation by \(3\) and both sides of the second equation by \(2\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} x=-\frac{4}{3} \text{ or } x=\frac{5}{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Extensions and cautions

    Principle 2.3.16. The Zero Product Property: three factor version.

    If \(a \cdot b \cdot c = 0\text{,}\) then \(a=0\) or \(b=0\) or \(c=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    This is an extended version of the Zero Product Property. There is a four factor version, and a five factor version, and so on.

    Example 2.3.17.

    Solve the equation \((x-1)(x+2)(x-3)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We have an equation where one side is a product and the other side is zero. The factors are \(x-1\text{,}\) \(x+2\text{,}\) and \(x-3\text{.}\) By the Zero Product Property, we have
    \begin{equation*} x-1=0 \text{ or } x+2=0 \text{ or } x-3=0. \end{equation*}
    So
    \begin{equation*} x=1 \text{ or } x=-2 \text{ or } x=3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Warning 2.3.18.

    Please note that the Zero Product Property does not apply when we have a sum. In other words, we cannot turn \((x+6)+(x+20)\) into \(x+6=0\) or \(x+20=0\text{.}\) The left side is a sum, not a product.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Finally, the techniques of this section are largely for quadratic equations. If we have an equation like \(3x+8=10x-12\text{,}\) then note that there is no quadratic term. Instead of the techniques from this section, examine Section 1.3, especially the technique from Strategy 1.3.43.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Summary

    1. The Zero Product Property applies to an equation when we have a product equal to zero.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The Zero Product Property does not apply when we do not have a product.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The Zero Product Property does not apply when we have a product, but that product is not equal to zero.
      🔗
      🔗
