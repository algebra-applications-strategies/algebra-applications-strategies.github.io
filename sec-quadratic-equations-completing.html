    Section 2.4 Quadratic equations: completing the square

    Objectives

    In this section, we learn how to:
    🔗
    1. Solve quadratic equations without a linear term.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Solve quadratic equations by completing the square when the qudratic coefficient is \(1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Solve quadratic equations by completing the square when the qudratic coefficient is not \(1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Special case: no linear term

    The last section gave a general method for solving quadratic equations using the method of factoring. We now introduce a method that works in the special case that our quadratic equation doesn’t have a linear term. As a reminder, in general, the kinds of terms that can appear are quadratic, linear, and constant. A quadratic term has \(x^2\text{,}\) while a linear term has \(x\) to the first power (with the power usually omitted), and a constant term is a term that does not have the variable at all. For example, \(8x^2 + 7x - 60=0\) has a quadratic term, linear term, and constant term. By contrast, \(8x^2-60=0\) has a quadratic term and a constant term, but no linear term.

    Strategy 2.4.1. Solving a quadratic equation with no linear term.

    • When does this strategy apply? This strategy applies in any equation we are asked to solve a quadratic equation that does not have a linear term.
      🔗
      🔗
    • How to apply the strategy
      1. Step 1: add and subtract on both sides so that the quadratic term and constant term appear on opposite sides of the equation.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. Step 2: square root both sides. (The first step in which square rooting occurs on both sides needs to have the plus-or-minus sign. While this symbol can be placed on either side, it is more convenient to put this on the side that does not have the variable.)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.2.

    Solve the equation \(x^2-9=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Since the equation has no linear term, we can apply Strategy 2.4.1. Adding \(9\) to both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x^2=9. \end{equation*}
    Step 2 of the strategy tells us to square root both sides and introduce a plus-or-minus sign:
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x^2} = \pm \sqrt{9}. \end{equation*}
    Note, it would be slightly incorrect if before aforementioned equation we had written
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x^2} = \sqrt{9}. \end{equation*}
    From \(\sqrt{x^2} = \pm \sqrt{9}\text{,}\) we simplify each side independently to get \(x= \pm3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Getting our final answer to be \(x=\pm3\) (which is read aloud "\(x\) equals plus or minus \(3\)") is equivalent to the final answer \(x=3 \text{ or } x = -3\) given earlier in (((xref without ref, first/last, or provisional attribute (check spelling)))).
    🔗

    Principle 2.4.3. Expectation when square rooting both sides of an equation.

    In the very first step in which the action of square rooting happens on both sides of an equation (even in the case when the action is indicated in the previous step’s work), a plus-or-minus sign must appear on one side or other side of the equation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Warning 2.4.4.

    A square root sign occurs when square rooting both sides of an equation, but when simplify an expression that has a square root symbol, a plus-or-minus sign is never artificially introduced into the problem.
    🔗
    🔗
    For example, when we simplified \(\sqrt{18}\) in Example 1.5.70, we never wrote the \(\pm\) symbol.
    🔗
    To be sure that we understand the concepts that we have introduced, let’s answer the following questions:

    Example 2.4.5.

    Simplify \(\sqrt{20}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(\sqrt{20}=\sqrt{4 \cdot 5} = \sqrt{4} \cdot \sqrt{5} = 2\sqrt{5}\)
    🔗
    We never performed the action of square rooting both sides of an equation, so that’s why there is no plus-or-minus sign anywhere in our work.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.6.

    Solve \(x^2 + 1= 20\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    Subtracting \(1\) on both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x^2=19. \end{equation*}
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x^2} = \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to
    \begin{equation*} x = \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    Subtracting \(1\) on both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x^2=19. \end{equation*}
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x = \pm \sqrt{19}. \end{equation*}
    In this answer, did not show the step that indicates the square rooting on both sides: just the result.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 3.
    We provide an answer without commentary:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + 1 = 20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^2 = 19 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x^2} = \pm\sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.7.

    Find the error in this written work:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + 1 = 20 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{x^2} = \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Answer.
    There are three equations. The middle equaton looks like someone first had written \(x^2=19\) but then afterward added work on top of that line by writing in square roots on both sides, but without having a plus-or-minus sign on that line of work. The first line of writing that has square rooting on both sides needs the plus-or-minus sign.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.8.

    Solve \((x-6)^2=19\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x-6 = \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    Adding \(6\) to both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x = 6 \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.9.

    Solve \((x-6)^2=19\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x-6 = \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    Adding \(6\) to both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x = 6 \pm \sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.10.

    Solve \((x+3)^2=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x+3 = \pm \sqrt{5} \end{equation*}
    Adding \(-3\) to both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x = -3 \pm \sqrt{5} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Area model: square-focused exploration

    Recall from Principle 1.1.40 that addition is represented by gluing sticks together, and from Principle 1.1.54 that multiplication is represented by the area of a rectangle.

    Example 2.4.11.

    Geometrically justify \((a+b)(c+d)=ac+ad+bc+bd\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The left side represents the area of a rectangle with sides \((a+b)\) and \((c+d)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    While the above is (generically) a rectangle, and each of the four panels are meant to represent rectangles, this section will focus on the geometry of squares.

    Example 2.4.13.

    Draw \(x \cdot x\text{,}\) also known as \(x^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The expression \(x \cdot x\) represents the area of a square with side length \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    While we can say "\(x\) to the power \(2\)" for \(x^2\text{,}\) the reason we can also say "\(x\) squared" is because \(x^2\) represents the area of a square with side length \(x\text{.}\)

    Example 2.4.15.

    Draw \((x+y)(x+y)\text{,}\) and show the contribution of each term in the expanded form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The expression \((x+y)(x+y)\) is also \((x+y)^2\) and this represents the area of a square with side length \(x+y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Instead of having two variables \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) this section will focus on \(x\) together with a number such as \(5\text{:}\)

    Example 2.4.17.

    Draw \((x+5)(x+5)\text{,}\) and show the contribution of each term in the expanded form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The expression \((x+5)(x+5)\) is also \((x+5)^2\) and this represents the area of a square with side length \(x+5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    It is important to note that we can also do this "backwards". We can start from a diagram, and then write the corresponding algebraic expression.

    Example 2.4.19.

    Write the corresponding algebraic expression for the following diagram:
    🔗
    Answer.
    In the picture above, we see \((x+7)^2 = (x+7)(x+7) = x^2 + 7x + 7x + 49 = x^2+14x + 49\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In the last example, we see the height and the width of the square are both \(x+7\text{.}\) The height and the width (which are both sums) correctly depict what Principle 1.1.40 says: adding is represented by gluing sticks together. There is a stick of mystery length (labeled \(x\)) glued to a second stick of length \(7\) to make a total length of \(x+7\text{.}\) We will take this example to introduce the useful terminology we will use in the rest of this section:
    • The length of the first stick is \(x\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The length of the second stick is \(7\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The linear coefficient is \(14\text{,}\) double the length of the second stick. (The linear coefficent refers to the number in front of \(x\text{,}\) excluding the \(x\) itself.)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The constant is \(49\text{,}\) the square of the length of the second stick.
      🔗
      🔗
    We will typically get quadratic expressions where we need to think of splitting up the linear term into two equal-sized pieces (to represent the rectangles drawn in the upper right and lower left). For example:

    Example 2.4.21.

    Rewrite \(x^2 + 20x + 100\) and draw the corresponding area model.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(x^2 + 20x + 100 = x^2 + 10x + 10x + 100\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In this example, the second stick has length \(10\text{,}\) the linear coefficient is \(20\text{,}\) and the constant is \(100\text{.}\) Note that there is no direct connection (in this or previous examples) between the linear coefficient and the constant. However, it always works out that doubling the length of the second stick gives the linear coefficient, and squaring the length of the second stick gives the constant.
    🔗
    Let’s examine a couple more quick explorations that will make the process we will learn go smoother!

    Example 2.4.23.

    In the diagram below, what is the missing amount of area that if added would complete the square?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The missing area is a square with side length \(3\text{,}\) so the missing area is \(3^2=9\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Before we complete the square, the diagram depicts \(x^2+3x+3x\text{,}\) and we see a linear coefficient of \(6\) (if we collect like terms on \(3x+3x\)). It is best to think of this as a two-step process:
    1. Step 1: take the linear coefficient \(6\) and divide by \(2\) to get the second stick \(3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Step 2: take the second stick \(3\) and square to get the constant \(9\text{,}\) which is the missing area.
      🔗
      🔗

    What should be added to \(x^2+16x\) to complete the square? Draw a picture of \(x^2+16x\) and draw a picture of the completed square after adding.

    Answer.

    Subsection First examples of completing the square

    Principle 2.4.31. Completing the square when the quadratic coefficient is \(1\).

    When the quadratic coefficient is \(1\) (so either a \(1\) is written in front of \(x^2\text{,}\) or nothing is written in front of \(x^2\text{,}\) which siginfies a hidden \(1\)) to complete the square:
    1. Identify the linear coefficient (the number in front of \(x\text{,}\) excluding the \(x\) itself).
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Divide the linear coefficient by \(2\) to get the second stick. (This is technically the "length of the second stick", but we will informally say "second stick".)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Square the second stick to get the constant that must be added to complete the square.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.32.

    Solve the equation \(x^2+12x=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The linear coefficient is \(12\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(6\text{,}\) and the constant that must be added to complete the square is \(36\text{.}\) Adding \(36\) to both sides of the equation gives
    \begin{equation*} x^2+12x+36=5+36. \end{equation*}
    Factoring on the left and simplifying on the right,
    \begin{equation*} (x+6)^2=41 \end{equation*}
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x+6=\pm\sqrt{41}. \end{equation*}
    Subtracting \(6\) from both sides gives
    \begin{equation*} x=-6\pm\sqrt{41} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.33.

    Solve \(x^2+6x=10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The linear coefficient is \(6\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(3\text{,}\) and the constant that must be added to complete the square is \(9\text{.}\) Adding \(9\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+6x+9=10+9. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x+3)^2=19 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+3=\pm\sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=-3\pm\sqrt{19} \end{equation*}
    Notice in the factored form \((x+3)^2\text{,}\) inside the parentheses, we see \(x\) plus the second stick. This will always happen!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The last example noted something very important:

    Principle 2.4.34.

    After adding the appropriate number to both sides, the factored form will always be \((x+\text{(second stick)})^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.35.

    Solve \(x^2+22x=-20\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The linear coefficient is \(22\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(11\text{,}\) and the constant that must be added to complete the square is \(121\text{.}\) Adding \(121\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+22x+121=-20+121. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x+11)^2=101 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+11=\pm\sqrt{101} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=-11\pm\sqrt{101} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.36.

    Solve \(x^2+14x+3=5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Seeing the \(3\) on the left side is visually distracting, so let’s start by subtracting \(3\) from both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+14x=2 \end{equation*}
    Now the problem has the same form as the previous examples, and we can complete the square. The linear coefficient is \(14\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(7\text{,}\) and the constant that must be added to complete the square is \(49\text{.}\) Adding \(49\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+14x+49=49-2. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x+7)^2=47 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+7=\pm\sqrt{47} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=-7\pm\sqrt{47} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The next example will feel a little strange because the linear coefficient is negative.

    Example 2.4.37.

    Solve \(x^2-10x-4=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    First, we will add \(4\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2-10x=6 \end{equation*}
    The linear coefficient is \(-10\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(-5\text{,}\) and so the constant is \(25\text{.}\) Because the linear coefficient is negative, the second stick is negative. While it is strange to say that the length of a stick is negative, let’s go with this and see if it works. Because the constant is found by squaring the second stick (and squaring a negative number gives a positive number), the constant is still positive. Adding \(25\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2-10x+25=6+25. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x-5)^2=31 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x-5=\pm\sqrt{31} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=5\pm\sqrt{31} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The point is that even with a negative linear coefficent (thus a negative second stick), the constant to add is still positive (and we do add the constant to both sides, instead of subtracting on both sides). So, while the description of the geometry is strange, the process works: we just need to stay absolutely consistent. We will always add the constant to both sides, which is always positive (even if the second stick is negative) since it is the result of squaring the second stick.

    Example 2.4.38.

    Solve \(x^2-6x+1=21\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{equation*} x^2-6x=20 \end{equation*}
    The linear coefficient is \(-6\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(-3\text{,}\) and the constant that must be added to complete the square is \(9\text{.}\) Adding \(9\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2-6x+9=20+9. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x-3)^2=29 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x-3=\pm\sqrt{29} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=3\pm\sqrt{29} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Our examples have so far had linear coefficients that are even for convenience:

    Example 2.4.39.

    Solve \(x^2+5x=7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The linear coefficient is \(5\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(\frac{5}{2}\text{,}\) so the constant is \((\frac{5}{2})^2\text{.}\) Recalling the formula \((\frac{a}{b})^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}\text{,}\) we can rewrite the constant \((\frac{5}{2})^2=\frac{5^2}{2^2}=\frac{25}{4}\text{.}\) So we add \(\frac{25}{4}\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+5x+\frac{25}{4}=7+\frac{25}{4}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x+\frac{5}{2})^2=\frac{53}{4} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+\frac{5}{2}=\pm\sqrt{\frac{53}{4}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=-\frac{5}{2}\pm\sqrt{\frac{53}{4}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    For this example, we had \(x^2+5x+\frac{25}{4}\) on the left side of one equation, and factored this as \((x+\frac{5}{2})^2\) in the next equation. This is consistent with Principle 2.4.34 for how the factoring should look, but we should also independently verify that this works: \((x+\frac{5}{2})^2 = (x+\frac{5}{2})(x+\frac{5}{2}) = x^2 + \frac{5}{2}x + \frac{5}{2}x + \frac{25}{4} = x^2 + 5x + \frac{25}{4}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The purpose of the preivous example is to show that even when the linear coefficient is odd, we can still complete the square. The second stick will be a fraction, and the constant will be a fraction, but the process works the same way. We had to make use of the formula \((\frac{a}{b})^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Final examples of completing the square

    In Principle 2.4.31, we saw what to do when the quadratic coefficient is \(1\text{.}\) What do we do when the quadratic coefficient is not \(1\text{?}\) For example, what do we do to solve \(3x^2+5x=6\text{,}\) where Principle 2.4.31 does not directly apply?
    🔗
    We can do some brief algebra (and be careful to follow valid fraction techniques) to get to a situation where Principle 2.4.31 does apply.

    Example 2.4.40.

    Solve \(3x^2+5x=6\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Because the quadratic coefficient is \(3\text{,}\) note that Principle 2.4.31 does not apply right now, but we will rewrite our problem so that we can apply the technique. Dividing both sides by \(3\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{3x^2+5x}{3}=\dfrac{6}{3} \end{equation*}
    We haven’t simplified our work above yet: we just wanted to point out that we had to divide the entire left side by \(3\text{,}\) not just the \(3x^2\) term. Because of the plus sign in the numerator, the \(3\)s on the left do not cancel. But we can apply the fraction addition formula to rewrite the left side (and will also use this step to simplify the right side).
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{3x^2}{3} + \dfrac{5x}{3} = 2. \end{equation*}
    Now that we no longer have a plus sign in the fraction (only a plus sign between fractions), we can cancel. We will also slightly rewrite \(\frac{5x}{3}\text{,}\) which is valid since \(\frac{5}{3}x = \frac{5}{3} \cdot \frac{x}{1} = \frac{5x}{3\cdot1}=\frac{5x}{3}\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + \frac{5}{3}x = 2. \end{equation*}
    Note the linear coefficient is \(\frac{5}{3}\text{,}\) not \(\frac{5}{3}x\text{.}\) (Remember that the linear coefficent is what is in front of \(x\text{,}\) but excludes \(x\) itself). So the second stick is \(\frac{5}{6}\text{,}\) because \(\frac{5}{3} \div 2 = \frac{5}{3} \div \frac21 = \frac{5}{3} \cdot \frac12 = \frac{5}{6}\text{.}\) The constant that must be added is \((\frac{5}{6})^2 = \frac{25}{36}\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + \frac{5}{3}x + \frac{25}{36} = 2 + \frac{25}{36}. \end{equation*}
    Factoring the left side as \(x\) plus the second stick, quantity squared, and simplifying the right side,
    \begin{equation*} (x+\frac{5}{6})^2 = \frac{97}{36}. \end{equation*}
    Square rooting both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x+\frac{5}{6} = \pm\sqrt{\frac{97}{36}}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+\frac{5}{6} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{97}}{\sqrt{36}}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+\frac{5}{6} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{97}}{6}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = - \frac{5}{6} \pm \frac{\sqrt{97}}{6}. \end{equation*}
    Now, we have isolated \(x\) and are done, but it is typical to combine the two fractions on the right side into a single fraction, since we are adding/subtracting fractions and they already have a common denominator:
    \begin{equation*} x = - \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{97}}{6}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    This example shows us that even when the quadratic coefficient is not \(1\text{,}\) we can still complete the square by first dividing both sides of the equation by the quadratic coefficient.

    Principle 2.4.41.

    Before identifying the linear coefficient and computing second stick and the constant, if the coefficient of \(x^2\) is not \(1\text{,}\) then divide both sides by the number that is in front of \(x^2\) first. Then Principle 2.4.31 will apply.
    🔗
    🔗
    Before we do another example to show this, a friendly word of advice is helpful. Examine both answers to this question:

    Example 2.4.42.

    Solve \(x^2+2x=15\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer 1.
    The linear coefficient is \(2\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(1\text{,}\) and the constant that must be added to complete the square is \(1\text{.}\) Adding \(1\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2+2x+1=15+1. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (x+1)^2=16 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+1=\pm\sqrt{16} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x+1=\pm4 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=-1\pm4 \end{equation*}
    We can leave our final answer like this, but we point out we have \(x=-5\) or \(x=3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer 2.
    Starting from \(x^2+2x=15\) and subtracting \(15\) on both sides,
    \begin{equation*} x^2+2x-15=0. \end{equation*}
    Factoring the left side,
    \begin{equation*} (x+5)(x-3)=0. \end{equation*}
    Applying the Zero Product Property,
    \begin{equation*} x+5=0 \text{ or } x-3=0. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=-5 \text{ or } x=3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    We include this example to show that there are multiple ways to solve a quadratic equation, and the method of completing the square is not always the most efficient. Completing the square always works on a quadratic equation, but sometimes factoring is less work.
    🔗
    We end by applying the process of completing the square to a quadratic equation where the coefficients are unknown, and represented by \(a\) and \(b\) and \(c\text{.}\) Instead of thinking of \(a\) and \(b\) and \(c\) a variables, think of them as mystery numbers. These are just numbers that are not known to us.

    Example 2.4.43.

    Solve the quadratic equation \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) by completing the square.
    🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    First, we will subtract \(c\) from both sides:
    \begin{equation*} ax^2+bx=-c. \end{equation*}
    The value of \(a\) might be \(1\text{,}\) but it might not. To make a generic argument that will always work, we follow Principle 2.4.41 and divide both sides by \(a\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{ax^2+bx}{a} = -\dfrac{c}{a}. \end{equation*}
    As written above, we cannot cancel the \(a\)s in the numerator and denominator on the left side. Rewrite the left side as the sum of two fractions.
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{ax^2}{a} + \dfrac{bx}{a} = -\dfrac{c}{a}. \end{equation*}
    Now we can cancel, and we also use the opportunity to rewrite the second term to have \(x\) outside of the fraction:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + \dfrac{b}{a}x = -\dfrac{c}{a}. \end{equation*}
    Now that the quadratic coefficient is \(1\text{,}\) we can apply Principle 2.4.31. The linear coefficient is \(\frac{b}{a}\text{,}\) recalling we only take what is next to \(x\text{,}\) but leave out \(x\) itself. Note \(\frac{b}{a} \div 2 = \frac{b}{a} \div \frac21 = \frac{b}{a} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{b}{2a}\text{,}\) so the second stick is \(\frac{b}{2a}\text{.}\) Since the second stick is \(\frac{b}{2a}\text{,}\) the constant is \((\frac{b}{2a})^2 = \frac{b^2}{(2a)^2} = \frac{b^2}{2^2a^2}=\frac{b^2}{4a^2}\text{.}\) To complete the square, we add \(\frac{b^2}{4a^2}\) to both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + \dfrac{b}{a}x + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2} = -\dfrac{c}{a} + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2}. \end{equation*}
    The left side factors, with the content in parentheses being \(x\) plus the second stick, and on the right side, we multiply the top and bottom of the first fraction by \(4a\) to get a common denominator:
    \begin{equation*} \left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = -\dfrac{4ac}{4a^2} + \dfrac{b^2}{4a^2}. \end{equation*}
    Rewriting just the right side, we have a single fraction, and we can write the numerator as \(-4ac+b^2\text{,}\) but we wrote \(b^2-4ac\) instead:
    \begin{equation*} \left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \dfrac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}. \end{equation*}
    Square rooting both sides:
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2} = \pm \sqrt{\dfrac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}}. \end{equation*}
    On the left side, square rooting and squaring undo each other. On the right side, we apply the square root of the fraction to the top and bottom:
    \begin{equation*} x + \dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4a^2}}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x + \dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{\sqrt{4}\sqrt{a^2}}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x + \dfrac{b}{2a} = \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}. \end{equation*}
    Subtracting \(\frac{b}{2a}\) from both sides:
    \begin{equation*} x = -\dfrac{b}{2a} \pm \dfrac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}. \end{equation*}
    Because the two fractions on the right side have a common denominator, we can combine them into a single fraction:
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The work from the previous example justifies a famous formula for quadratic equations, called the quadratic formula:

    Principle 2.4.44.

    The solutions to the quadratic equation \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) are given by the formula
    \begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The expression \(b^2-4ac\) found inside the square root is called the discriminant:
    • If \(b^2-4ac < 0\text{,}\) then \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) has zero real solutions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(b^2-4ac=0\text{,}\) then \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) has one real solution.
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(b^2-4ac > 0\text{,}\) then \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) has two real solutions.
      🔗
      🔗

    Subsection Summary

    1. To solve a quadratic equation with no linear coefficient, get one term on each side, then square root both sides (and include \(\pm\) in the first step that introduces square rooting both sides).
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If the coefficient of \(x^2\) is not \(1\text{,}\) divide both sides by the coefficient of \(x^2\) before completing the square.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If the coefficient of \(x^2\) is \(1\text{,}\) identify the linear coefficient (the value in front of \(x\text{,}\) but excluding \(x\) itself), divide it by \(2\) to get the second stick, and square the second stick to get the constant that must be added to both sides to complete the square.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. In its factored form, we will have a certain quantity squared, and that quantity will be \(x\) plus the second stick.
      🔗
